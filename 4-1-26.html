    Section 2 4-1-26

    Characteristic, Redefinition of Field Adjunctions, and Minimal Polynomials.
    🔗

    Proposition 2.1. Primality of Characteristic.

    \(\textrm{char } F\) is either \(0\) or prime for field \(F\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Assume \(\textrm{char } F = n\) for some positive, composite \(n\text{.}\) Let \(\phi: \Z \rightarrow F\text{.}\) It follows that \(\ker \phi = (n)\text{.}\) By the Fourth Isomorphism Theorem, there is a bijection between the ideals of \(\Z\) containing \((n)\) and the ideals of \(\textrm{im } \phi\text{.}\) Since \(n\) is composite by assumption, assume \(n = ab\) for integers \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\) It follows that \((n) \subset (a)\) and \((n) \subset (b)\text{.}\) However, this implies the existence of more than two ideals in \(F\text{,}\) which contradicts the property that fields have only two ideals.
    🔗
    Thus, if \(\textrm{char } F\) is nonzero, then \(\textrm{char } F\) must be prime.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.2. \(\F_3(\sqrt{2})\).

    We want to define objects like \(\Q(\sqrt{2})\) without a larger "ambient" field like \(\R\text{.}\) Thus, define \(L = \F_3(\sqrt{2})\) such that
    1. \(L/\F_3\text{.}\)
      🔗
    2. \(L\) contains \(\theta\) such that \(\theta^2 = 2\text{.}\)
      🔗
    3. \(L\) is minimal.
      🔗
    🔗
    Note that \(L \ne \F_3\text{.}\) \(0^2 = 0\text{,}\) \(1^2 = 1\text{,}\) and \(2^2 = 4 = 1\text{,}\) so there is no element in \(\F_3\) whose square is \(2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The above properties suggest that \(L = \F_3[x]/(x^2 - 2)\) where \(\theta\) is the image of \(x\) in \(L\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 2.3. Irreducibility of \(p(x)\) for Field \(F[x]/(p(x))\).

    For any field \(F\) and any polynomial \(p(x) \in F[x]\) of positive degree, the ring \(F[x]/(p(x))\) is a field if and only if \(p(x)\) is irreducible.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.4.

    \(x^2 - 2\) is irreducible. Assume \(x^2 - 2\) is reducible so \(x^2 - 2 = (x - a)(x - b) = 0\text{.}\) However, there is no \(a, b \in \F_3\) such that \(a^2 = 2\) or \(b^2 = 2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 2.5. Degree of \(F[x]/(p(x))\).

    Let \(F\) be a field and assume \(p(x) \in F[x]\text{.}\) Then, \([F[x]/(p(x)) : F] = \deg p\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Put \(L = F[x]/(p(x))\text{,}\) let \(\theta = \pi(x)\)> for the quotient map \(\pi: F[x] \rightarrow L\text{,}\) and assume \(n = \deg p\text{.}\) We will show that \(L\) has basis \(B = \{1, \theta, \theta^2, \ldots, \theta^{n - 1}\}\text{.}\)
    1. \(B\) is linearly independent. Assume there exist coefficients \(a_{n - 1}, \ldots, a_0\text{,}\) such that \(a_{n - 1}\theta^{n - 1} + \cdots + a_0 = 0\text{.}\) Let \(q(x) = a_{n - 1}x^{n - 1} + \cdots + a_0\text{.}\) It must be true that \(\pi(q(x)) = 0\text{.}\) Then, \(q(x) \in (p(x))\text{.}\) However, \(\deg q < n\) and \(\deg p = n\text{.}\) The only \(q\) satisfying these properties is \(q(x) = 0\) so all \(a_0, \ldots, a_{n - 1}\) must be 0, as required.
      🔗
    2. \(B\) spans \(L\text{.}\) \(F[x]\) is spanned by \(\{1, x, x^2, \ldots\}\) so \(L\) is spanned by \(\{1, \theta, \theta^2, \ldots\}\text{.}\) Now, assume without loss of generality that \(p\) is monic and \(p(x) = x^2 + a_{n - 1}x^{n - 1} + \cdots + a_0\text{.}\) We have
      \begin{equation*} \theta^n = -a_{n - 1}\theta^{n - 1} - \cdots - a_0 \end{equation*}
      since \(\pi(p(x)) = 0\text{.}\) Consider \(\theta^{n + 1}\text{.}\) It follows
      \begin{equation*} \theta^{n + 1} = -a_{n - 1}\theta^n - \cdots - a_0\theta. \end{equation*}
      It follows, by induction,
      \begin{equation*} \theta^{n + k} = -a_{n - 1}\theta^{n + k} - \cdots - a_0\theta^k\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Therefore, every \(\theta^n, \theta^{n + 1}, \ldots\) is a linear combination in the span of \(B\) and
      \begin{equation*} \langle B\rangle = \langle 1, \ldots, \theta^n\rangle = \langle 1, \ldots, \theta^{n + 1}\rangle = \langle 1, \theta, \theta^2, \ldots\rangle = L. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    🔗
    Consequently, \(B\) is a basis of \(L\) so \([L:F] = n\text{,}\) as required.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.6. Alternative Definitions of Prior Adjunctions\(\F_3(\sqrt{2})\).

    We can now say that \(\F_3(\sqrt{2}) \cong \F_3[x]/(x^2 - 2)\) so \([\F_3(\sqrt{2}):\F_3] = 2\text{.}\) Furthermore, \(\F_3[x]/(x^2 - 2)\) is minimal since \(2\) is the smallest basis greater than \(1\) and by the Tower Law, for \(L/K/F\text{,}\) \([L:F] = [L:K] \cdot [K:F]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Additionally, \(\{a + b\sqrt{2} \mid a, b \in \Q\} \cong \Q(\sqrt{2}) \cong \Q[x]/(x^2 - 2)\text{.}\) Of course, this is still not prove and we must show that \(\{a + b\sqrt{2} \mid a, b \in \Q\} \cong \Q[x]/(x^2 - 2)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.7. Minimal Polynomial.

    Suppose \(L/F\) is a field extension and let \(\alpha \in L\text{.}\) The minimal polynomial for \(\alpha\) is the unique polynomial \(p(x) \in F[x]\) of minimum degree such that \(p(\alpha) = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 2.8. Properties of Minimal Polynomial.

    Let \(\iota: F[x] \rightarrow F(\alpha)\) where \(\iota(x) = \alpha\text{.}\) Define \(q \in F[x]\) such that \(\ker \iota = (q(x))\) (since \(F[x]\) is a PID) where \(q\) is monic. Then, \(q(x)\) is unique and \(q(x)\) is the minimal polynomial of \(\alpha\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    1. \(q(x)\) is unique. Assume \(\ker \iota = (r(x))\text{.}\) Then \(q(x) = a(x) \cdot r(x)\text{.}\) Also, \(r(x) = b(x) \cdot q(x)\text{.}\) Thus,
      \begin{equation*} r(x) = b(x) \cdot (a(x) \cdot r(x)) = (b(x) \cdot a(x)) \cdot r(x)\text{,} \end{equation*}
      so \(b(x)\) must be a unit and \(b^{-1} = a\text{.}\) The only units of \(F[x]\) are the scalars. Since \(r(x)\) and \(q(x)\) are both monic, we must have \(b(x) = 1\text{,}\) so \(r(x) = q(x)\text{.}\)
      🔗
    2. Let \(p(x)\) be the minimal polynomial of \(\alpha\text{.}\) \(q(\alpha) = 0\text{.}\) This is since \(\iota(q(x)) = q(\alpha) = 0\) as \(q \in \ker \iota\text{.}\) Therefore, \(q(x) \mid p(x)\text{.}\) However, \(q\) and \(p\) are both monic and \(p\) has minimal degree. Therefore, \(q(x) = p(x)\text{.}\)
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Corollary 2.9.

    Let \(p \in F[x]\) be the minimal polynomial of \(\alpha\) for some \(\alpha \in L/F\text{.}\) Define \(\iota: F[x]/(p(x)) \rightarrow F(\alpha)\) by \(\iota(\overline{p(x)}) = p(\alpha)\text{.}\) By the First Isomorphism Theorem, \(\iota\) is an isomorphism and \(F[x]/(p(x)) \cong F(\alpha)\text{.}\) Additionally, we have \(\alpha \in \textrm{im}(\iota)\) and \(F(\alpha)\) is minimal among the fields containing \(F\) and \(\alpha\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.10.

    Let \(F = \Q\text{,}\) \(L = \R\text{,}\) and \(\alpha = \sqrt{2}\text{.}\) Then, \(\Q[x]/(x^2 - 2) \cong \Q(\sqrt{2})\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.11. Nonexistence of Minimal Polynomials.

    Note that minimal polynomials need not exist. For instance, \(\pi \in \R\) has no minimal polynomial in \(\Q[x]\) by the work of Lindemann in 1882. Thus, no monic polynomial in \(\Q\) vanishes at \(\pi\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.12. Algebraic and Transcendental.

    Given \(\alpha \in L\text{,}\) \(L/F\text{,}\) \(\alpha\) is algebraic ovr \(F\) if there is a nonzero polynomial in \(F[x]\) vanishing at \(\alpha\text{.}\) Otherwise, \(\alpha\) is called transcendental.
