    Section 1 3-31-26

    Definition 1.1. Subfield.

    Given a field \((F, +, \cdot)\text{,}\) a subfield of \(F\) is a subset of \(F\text{,}\) closed under \(+\text{,}\) \(-\text{,}\) \(\cdot\text{,}\) \(/\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.2. Extension.

    An extension of \(F\) is a field \(K\) containing \(F\) as a subfield. Denoted \(K/F\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 1.3. Injectivity of Field Homomorphisms.

    Any homomorphism of fields are injective.
    🔗
    Note that we only require the codomain of the homomorphism to be a nonzero ring.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(F\) and \(K\) be fields and assume \(\iota: F \rightarrow K\) is a ring homomorphism. Take \(I = \ker \iota\) an ideal of \(F\text{.}\) Assume \(I = 0\text{.}\) When, \(\iota\) is injective, and we are done. Now assume \(I \ne 0\text{.}\) By the properties of fields, it must be the case that \(I = F\) and \(\iota(I) = \iota(F) = 0\text{.}\) However, this contradicts \(1 \ne 0\) in \(K\text{.}\) Thus, \(\ker \iota = 0\text{,}\) so \(\iota\) is injective.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 1.4. Field Homomorphism Codomain Structures.

    Take \(\iota: F \hookrightarrow K\text{.}\) \(\iota\) turns \(K\) into an \(F\)-algebra. It follows that \(K\) is an \(F\)-vector space.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.5. Degree.

    The degree of the extension \(K/F\) is the degree of \(K\) as an \(F\)-vector space.
    🔗
    Denoted \([K:F]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.6. Degree of \(\C/\R\).

    \(\C = \{a + bi \mid a, b \in \R\}\text{.}\) Thus, \(\{1, i\}\) is a basis for \(\C/\R\) so \([\C:\R]\) = 2.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.7. Degree of \(\R/\Q\).

    If \(|\Q| = N\text{,}\) we have \(|\R| = 2^N\text{,}\) as the cardinality of \(\R\) has the same cardinality as the power set of \(\Q\text{.}\) Since the power set of \(\Q\) has strictly greater cardinality than \(\Q\) by Cantor’s Theorem, it follows that \(\R\) is uncountable.
    🔗
    Now, assume \(\R\) has a finite basis. Then, the set of linear combinations of the finite basis with coefficients in \(\Q\) would be countable, contradicting the uncountability of \(\R\text{.}\) Consequently, any basis of \(\R\) as a \(\Q\)-vector space must be infinite, so \([\R:\Q] = \infty\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.8. Intermediate Field.

    An intermediate field of \(K/F\) is a field \(L\) such that \(F \subseteq L\) and \(K\) is an extension of \(L\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.9. \(\Q(\sqrt 2)\).

    \(\sqrt 2 \in \R\text{.}\) Let \(\Q(\sqrt 2) = \{a + b \sqrt 2 \mid a, b \in \Q\}\text{.}\) Then, \(\Q(\sqrt 2)\) is a field.
    🔗

    Proof.

    The field axioms for addition, multiplication, and distributivity are easily shown.
    🔗
    Additionally, we have \((a + b \sqrt 2)^{-1} = \frac{1}{a^2 - 2b^2}(a - b\sqrt{2})\text{.}\) The denominator \(a^2 - 2b^2\) must be nonzero for nonzero \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\) This is clearly true if only one \(a\) or \(b\) is nonzero. Thus, suppose both \(a\) and \(b\) are nonzero. If \(a^2 - 2b^2 = 0\text{,}\) then \(a^2 = 2b^2\) which implies \(\sqrt{2}\) is rational. Consequently, \(\Q(\sqrt{2})\) is closed under multiplicative inverses.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.10. Field Adjunction.

    Take field extension \(K/F\) and let \(\alpha \in K\text{.}\) Let the field adjunction \(F(\alpha)\) be the minimal field \(L\text{,}\) intermediate to \(K/F\text{,}\) containing \(\alpha\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 1.11. Intersections of Fields.

    Given fields \(F_1, F_2 \subseteq K\text{,}\) \(F_1 \cap F_2\) is also a field.
    🔗
    Note that this generalizes to arbitrary intersections.
    🔗
    🔗

    Corollary 1.12. Intersections and Field Adjunctions.

    \(F(\alpha)\) is the intersection of all intermediate fields of \(K/F\) containing \(\alpha\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.13. Characteristic.

    Given a field \(F\text{,}\) the characteristic of \(F\text{,}\) denoted \(\textrm{char } F\text{,}\) is the minimal positive integer \(n\) such that \(\underset{n \textrm{ times}}{\underbrace{1 + 1 + \cdots + 1}} = 0\text{.}\) If there is no such \(n\text{,}\) then \(\textrm{char } F = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Note that characteristic is preserved under field extensions and subfields.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.14.

    Ring \(\F_p = \Z/p\Z\) is a field for \(p\) prime.
